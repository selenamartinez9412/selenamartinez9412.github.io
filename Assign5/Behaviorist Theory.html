Behaviorist Theory

The Behaviorist Theory is a concept we have all encountered, whether we realize it or not. This theory theorizes that learned behaviors are enforced through a series of rewards and punishments. The Behaviorist theories is common when discussing behavior management and behavioral therapy. This theory is best implemented with concepts that have an absolute desired outcome rather than a creative topic with more student freedom. The purpose of behaviorism is to reinforce positive habits through conditioning and repetition.

Examples of when to use behaviorism:

  • Teaching a classroom to react to a certain gesture given by a teacher like a “call and response”.
  • Learning the fundamentals of playing an instrument or rules to a sports game. This is because the rules and posture while performing these tasks have a specific way to accomplish success.
  • Memorizing multiplication by repetition and reward.
  • Learning appropriate behaviors through repetition. An example of this would be a teacher reinforcing positive behaviors by acknowledging a students kindness.
  • Learning a new language through an app such as Duolingo, which teaches language through repetition, correction, and positive reinforcement.


Things to Remember When Utilizing Positive Reinforcement

  • The teacher/instructor’s role is to facilitate an activity that allows students to repeat the skill being learned continuously.
  • The students’ behavioral responses will be shaped by continuous feedback.
  • The learner should have a clear understanding of what is expected of them as soon as possible.
  • Repetition is key.
  • Continuous reinforcement can seem exhausting, but after the behavior is learned, reinforcing only partially will suffice.
